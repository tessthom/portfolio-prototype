THINGS I LOVE

NATIVE CSS

Honestly, if I could spend every day styling content I'd be happy as a clam. I love learning from folks like Kevin Powell and dialing in my approach a little more with each project. I'm slowly building reusable component styles for myself with the goal of making my own library. CSS gets such a bad wrap but is full of so many powerful features nowadays that it's hard to imagine reaching for bloated frameworks

CRAFTSMANSHIP

I come to web development from a career spent building objects to exacting specifications. In the world of studio furniture one degree or a 64th of an inch matters. As a set builder, or boat maker, lives often depended on the quality of my work. Nothing leaves the shop sub-par. Even when I knew nobody would ever see some little detail under a chair or on stage from 30' away and in the dark, it mattered to me. I don't cut corners. It's just not who I am. Not then and not now.

STAYING INSPIRED

I've worn a lot of 'maker' hats in life. Between sculture, installation art, illustration, furnituremaking, ceramics, timberframing and scenic carpentry, I've been lucky enough to work with many mediums and be exposed to myriad brilliant artists and craftspeople. I cannot overstate how much of a thrill it is to find inspiration in other artforms and apply them to web design. Recently I've been obsessed with painter Sophie Treppendahl's mastery of light. Check her out. You'll be glad you did.

VANILLA JS, BABY

Outside of enterprise or e-commerce, I love leaving abstraction on the shelf in favor of plain old JS. I've also tried my best to focus on this foundational skill rather than leaning too far into frameworks as a student. If web dev is anything like woodworking, core skill comprehension makes noobs more adaptable down the line.

UTOPIAN DESIGN

When I first came across Utopia.fyi I imagined my old studio art profs smiling down from a classically proportioned cloud. Total game changer.

BIAS LIGHTING

Installing quality bias lighting (LED strips set to emit a particular quality of white light at the wall behind displays) behind my main monitor has significantly cut down on eye strain and in turn, migraines, for me. Not to mention it helps me percieve color as accurately as possible. If you haven't heard of it and are interested, check out biaslighting.com. You'll find a lot of LED strips sold as "bias lighting" elsewhere, but these folks make the real deal. 10/10 highly recommend for TVs, gaming, and any kind of design-related work.
